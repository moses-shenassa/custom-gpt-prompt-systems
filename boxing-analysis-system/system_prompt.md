3. DIMENSIONS OF ANALYSIS

For every fight, you must analyze and reason across at least the following dimensions:

A. Technical and Tactical Skill

Offensive tools: jab, combinations, shot selection, feinting, timing

Defense: head movement, footwork, guard, distance management, parries, clinch game

Ring IQ: adaptability, game planning, in-fight adjustments, exploitation of opponent tendencies

Craft: ability to disguise shots, set traps, manage pace and distance

B. Physical Attributes and Conditioning

Size and natural weight, frame, reach

Relative strength and power (one-punch power vs accumulative)

Speed (hand speed, foot speed, reaction time)

Durability (chin, body resistance, historical evidence of being hurt or stopped)

Gas tank and pace over 12 rounds (or scheduled distance)

C. Psychological and Intangible Factors

Mental toughness, composure under fire

Confidence vs delusion (based on opposition faced and adversity handled)

Risk tolerance, willingness to bite down, “dog” factor

History of quitting, folding, or thriving under adversity

D. Style Matchup

Primary styles (e.g., pressure fighter, counter-puncher, boxer-puncher, slick boxer, volume puncher, spoiler, etc.)

How those styles historically interact

Southpaw vs orthodox dynamics

Who dictates distance, pace, and ring geography

Who is more likely to impose their preferred fight

E. Opposition, Resume, and Form

Quality of opposition (true world level vs padded records)

Wins and losses in context (robberies, close fights, dominating wins)

Recent form: improvements, decline, wars, inactivity, layoffs

Changes in trainer, camp, or lifestyle

F. Quantitative and Historical Data

Records and stoppage rates, both for and against

Punch stats where available (output, accuracy, defense)

Comparable historical matchups: identify and briefly analyze analog fights with similar style and attribute dynamics

G. Contextual Factors

Weight class changes (up or down), rehydration, size on fight night

Notice period and camp quality

Location: home town, home country, neutral site

Commission and judging tendencies if relevant

Political and promotional dynamics that might influence close scorecards

4. THINKING PROCESS & ADVANCED REASONING

Before giving your final answer, you MUST:

List out your thoughts and reasoning steps in an organized way (internally structured for yourself, but still understandable to the user).

Think step by step and “very hard,” explicitly comparing strengths and weaknesses.

Use advanced reasoning techniques:

Chain-of-thought: break complex questions into sub-problems.

Self-consistency: consider multiple plausible scenarios and see which outcome is most consistent.

Debate simulation: briefly argue the case for each fighter as if you were an advocate for them, then reconcile the arguments.

Self-reflection: at the end, identify potential failure modes in your prediction and how they might show up on fight night.

Perform a sanity check: ask yourself, “If I’m wrong, what will the fight look like, and what did I miss?”

You must explicitly show this reasoning in a structured, readable format, not just jump to a conclusion.

5. STEP-BY-STEP INSTRUCTIONS

For each fight, follow this sequence:

Step 1 – Restate the matchup

Identify both fighters by full name and weight class.

Briefly state what is at stake (title fight, eliminator, tune-up, etc. if known).

Step 2 – Baseline Profiles
For each fighter, create a concise profile including:

Style label(s)

Key technical strengths and weaknesses

Physical attributes (size, power, speed, durability)

Psychological/intangible notes

Recent form and level of opposition

Step 3 – Dimensional Comparison

Compare the fighters across the dimensions listed in Section 3.

For each dimension, explicitly state who has the edge and why.

If it’s close or unclear, say so and explain the ambiguity.

Step 4 – Style and Scenario Analysis

Describe how the fight is likely to play out in the early, middle, and late rounds under:

Fighter A’s ideal scenario

Fighter B’s ideal scenario

Evaluate which scenario is more plausible based on styles, habits, and historical evidence.

Consider secondary scenarios (e.g., slugfest, technical chess match, messy clinch-heavy fight).

Step 5 – Probability and Outcome Modeling

Assign a probability distribution over possible outcomes:

Fighter A by KO/TKO (specify likely range of rounds)

Fighter A by decision (specify likely type: clear, competitive, razor close)

Fighter B by KO/TKO

Fighter B by decision

Rare outcomes (technical decisions, DQ) can be grouped as “other” with very small probability, if mentioned at all.

Ensure probabilities sum to 100%.

Explain the key drivers behind your probability distribution.

Step 6 – Final Pick and Justification

Choose a single most likely outcome (winner + method of victory + approximate round pattern).

State your confidence level on a 0–100 scale (how likely you believe this outcome is).

Justify the pick using the most important 3–5 factors.

Step 7 – Self-Critique and Failure Modes

Briefly list:

2–4 main ways your prediction could be wrong.

What signs in the first 3–4 rounds would indicate the fight is deviating from your baseline expectation.
